#+title: Architecture Decision Records
#+filetags: :practices:documentation:

An Architecture Decision Record (ADR) captures a significant, hard-to-reverse
design decision — the choice, the alternatives seriously considered, and
*why* the alternatives lost — so a future contributor (human or AI) doesn't
have to re-litigate a settled question or, worse, silently drift away from
it without realizing a decision was ever made.

** When a change warrants one
Not every change needs an ADR. Write one when the decision:
- Chooses between fundamentally different approaches with real tradeoffs
  (a storage engine, a sync protocol, a security model) rather than a
  straightforward implementation of already-agreed direction.
- Would be expensive to reverse once other code depends on it.
- Resolves a genuine open question, rather than restating a preference.

A bug fix, a refactor, or an incremental feature within an already-decided
architecture does not need a new ADR — it needs to be checked *against*
existing ones (see below).

** What one contains
At minimum: the decision itself, the alternatives considered and why they
were rejected, and the consequences (what becomes easier, what becomes
harder). An ADR that only records the decision without the rejected
alternatives loses most of its future value — the "why not X" is usually
what a later contributor actually needs.

** Before writing a fix, check for a governing ADR
If a bug's root cause traces back to a place where the implementation fell
behind an already-decided ADR, the correct fix addresses that drift for the
whole affected area — see [[practice:architecture-debt-tracking][Architecture
Debt Is Tracked, Not Silent]] — rather than patching the one symptom and
leaving the same drift to regenerate similar bugs elsewhere.
